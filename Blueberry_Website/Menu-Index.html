Cyanococcus, better known by its notable name
Blueberry, is a small edible blue plant, member
of the heather family of Vaccinium. Usually best
farmed in a humid climate, and acidic soil,
previously seen with no potential due to belief
that it couldn’t be domesticated is now produced
across 5 continents in 1 billion lb. The blueberries
are where it is now due to the determination and
minds of Elizabeth Coleman White and Fredrick Vernon
Coville.

Elizabeth Coleman White, born on October 5, 1871 in New lisbon, New Jersey, to a family of farmers. Her father was the manager of a 600-acre cranberry farm, growing up she spent her childhood summers in that farm helping out on her parents. Her time in the farm sparked her interest in cultivating blueberries, at the time it was common knowledge that wild blueberries could not be cultivated due to diverse in its size and sweetness.

Frederick Vernon Coville, born on March 23, 1867 in Preston, New York, to Joseph Addison Coville, and his wife Lydia. He attended Cornell University, and graduated with a B.A. For Most of his career he served as Assistant Botanist at the United States Department of Agriculture, to later become a Chief botanist in the same department. In 1910, he began his research on blueberries. In 1911, he began to collaborate on the project with Elizabeth White and by 1916, they succeeded in cultivating blueberries.

Planting your Blueberry Bush

Blueberries are ideally planted during spring
for best result.

  • Before planting, select a spot that is far
    away from any trees or buildings, that will
    prevent any sunlight from reaching your plants.
  • It's important to understand the type of soil
    your plants prefer, Blueberries well tolerate
    acidic soil and thrive heavily once it's paired
    with well drained soil.
  • Blueberries are often planted in multiple bushes,
    space them 6 feet apart, and allow them to grow
    individually.
  • Once your bush has settled, pour mulch over the
    roots to conserve moisture, and prevent weed growth.
  • Water your bush up to 1 - 2” per week during
    its growing season, and once it starts to ripen,
    double the amount of water.
